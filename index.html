Section 2

The Adoption Mirage

Consultancy surveys report 78–88% AI “adoption.” Independent data tells a radically different story. The gap is not an accident — it’s a definitional choice.

Why the numbers diverge: Consultancy surveys ask senior executives at large enterprises whether anyone, anywhere in their organisation uses AI for anything — a threshold so low that a single employee using ChatGPT counts. They survey their own clients and prospects (selection bias) and rely on self-reported answers (no CEO says “we’re behind on AI”). Independent bodies like the OECD measure all firms with 10+ employees and ask whether AI is embedded in core business operations. The definitions are not equivalent — and the consultancies never flag this when headlining their numbers.

Five Firms, One Playbook

Each consultancy follows the same pattern: publish alarming forecasts and reassuring counter-narratives in parallel. The common thread is not the message — it’s the outcome: every position generates a billable engagement. Tap each firm to see their sharpest contradictions.

The pattern that repeats across all five firms:
Alarming forecast → sells transformation engagements   |   Reassuring counter-narrative → sells augmentation & change management   |   Both → sell governance & risk consulting

The Hype-to-Revenue Flywheel

Whether by design or by the natural incentives of the consulting business model, the same pattern repeats across all five firms:

1
Publish bold thought leadership. “$4.4 trillion opportunity.” “AI could automate 60-70% of work.” “Tipping point reached.” Headlines that generate media coverage and board-level attention.
2
Urgency builds in the C-suite. CEOs and boards read the headlines. Fear of missing out. Fear of disruption. Fear of being left behind. “We need to act now.”
3
Named AI practices are ready. Deloitte GenAI Practice. EY.ai. QuantumBlack. KPMG Workbench. PwC agent OS. All five firms — branded, staffed, ready for sales conversations.
4
Transformation engagements follow. Strategy workshops. Readiness assessments. AI roadmaps. Pilot programmes. Each one feeds the next.
5
When pilots stall, “reset” narratives emerge. “Value is harder than expected.” “Deeper organisational surgery needed.” The revised narrative supports the next round of consulting.
6
The cycle repeats. New surveys show progress is slow. Recommend scaling. Recommend governance. Recommend reskilling. Each phase generates demand for the next.

The Independent Record

Four independent sources — none funded by consultancies — provide the ground truth. Here is what they found.

OECD · Official Statistics 2025
20.2%
of firms with 10+ employees used AI in 2025 — up from 8.7% in 2023. Growth is real, but from a low base.
52% of large firms use AI vs 17.4% of small firms. AI adoption has a massive size gradient.
In G7 countries, AI use in core business functions producing goods and services was below 10% (range: 1.9% Japan to 6.1% US).
Stanford HAI · AI Index 2025
<10%
Most commonly reported AI cost savings. Revenue increases most commonly below 5%. Gains cluster at modest levels.
The “78% adoption” headline figure comes from McKinsey’s own survey included in the AI Index — not from independent measurement.
Even where value is claimed, reported gains are consistent with early-stage diffusion where deployments are incremental.
Gartner · Multiple Surveys 2024
49%
of organisations cite difficulty estimating and demonstrating business value as the top barrier to AI adoption.
Only 9% classified as AI-mature. Only 48% of AI projects reach production. Average prototype-to-production: 8 months.
Gartner predicts 30% of GenAI projects will be abandoned after proof-of-concept by end of 2025. Deployment costs: $5M–$20M.
Public Earnings Transcripts · 2024–2026
Concentrated
Concrete, numeric ROI claims appear mostly in adtech and vertical SaaS: DoubleVerify reports $4 return per $1 invested; Toast cites 20x return on AI-enabled ads.
Hyperscalers (Microsoft, Alphabet) justify AI via aggregate demand and capex narratives, not unit-economics ROI.
Meta distinguishes between mature AI returns in core ads business and earlier-stage GenAI not expected to drive revenue near-term — a distinction consultancies rarely make.
The punchline: If AI were delivering the transformative ROI that consultancies headline, CEOs would be boasting about it to shareholders every quarter. They are not. The silence in earnings calls is the strongest evidence that the trillions remain theoretical.
Section 7

What This Means

Documenting contradictions is not the point. Understanding what they cost — and what to do about it — is.

For Boards & C-Suites
Ask your consultancy for a single, internally consistent position on AI. If they cannot reconcile what their thought leadership says with what their project teams deliver, that is a red flag — not about AI, but about the advice you are paying for.

Demand independent benchmarks. When your consultancy cites “78% adoption,” ask: whose survey, what sample, what definition of adoption? Cross-reference against OECD and Gartner data before committing eight-figure transformation budgets.

Separate the signal from the sales cycle. AI is a real technology with real applications. But the gap between consultancy headlines and independent evidence suggests the urgency is overstated. Match your investment pace to observed ROI, not to forecasted trillion-dollar ceilings.
For Policymakers & Regulators
Consultancy adoption figures shape policy. When governments hear “88% of firms use AI,” they legislate for an AI-saturated economy. When the OECD says 20%, the policy response should be different. AI strategy, workforce programmes, and regulatory frameworks built on inflated baselines will misallocate resources.

Require source transparency. When consultancy surveys are cited in policy documents, require disclosure of sample composition, survey methodology, and the definition of “adoption” being used. The Stanford AI Index circular citation shows how easily consultancy data enters the policy bloodstream as “independent” evidence.

Commission independent measurement. Government statistical agencies — not firms with a commercial stake in the outcome — should be the primary source for AI adoption data used in policymaking.
A necessary caveat: None of this means AI is without value, or that consultancies deliver no useful work. Firms may publish contradictory thought leadership while still executing competent implementations for clients. The problem is not the technology — it is that the public narrative around the technology is shaped by organisations with a structural incentive to overstate both the urgency and the difficulty, because both generate revenue. Readers deserve to know that when they read the next “AI will transform everything” report from a firm that earns billions from AI transformation projects.
Methodology & Sources
This investigation is based on original analysis of 100+ publications from McKinsey, Deloitte, EY, KPMG, and PwC (2022–2026), cross-referenced against independent data from OECD official statistics, the Stanford HAI AI Index Report, Gartner surveys, and public company earnings transcripts. All claims are directly sourced. The full research corpus includes 47 McKinsey sources, 36 Deloitte sources, 26 EY sources, 14 KPMG sources, 6 PwC sources, and 16 independent data sources.
A note on fairness: Consultancies are large, decentralised organisations. Different service lines, geographies, and publication teams may legitimately hold different views. Some contradictions are temporal (the evidence evolved) and some are definitional (different metrics). We acknowledge this throughout the analysis. But when every contradiction — temporal or otherwise — opens a new revenue stream, and when the same pattern repeats across all five firms independently, organisational complexity becomes less an explanation and more a feature of a business model where consistency has no commercial upside.
